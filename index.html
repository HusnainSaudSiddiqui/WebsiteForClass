Expendable Childhoods?

Child Labor and American War

We remember the generals and the presidents. We rarely remember the twelve-year-olds in factories or the teenagers in fields whose labor kept wartime economies running. When a nation fights "for the future" but leans on the bodies and time of its children, what does that really mean?

The Question Guiding This Project

When you put the history of child labor in the United States next to U.S. policy on child soldiers, a hard question appears:

Whose childhood has been treated as expendable, and when does that finally change?

This website explores that question in two directions: childhoods sacrificed in American factories and fields during wartime, and childhoods endangered abroad through armed conflicts that still receive U.S. military support.

Hidden in Plain Sight: Child Labor at Home

Child labor in American history is often treated as something foreign or distant in time, but expert histories show that it was deeply rooted in the United States well into the twentieth century. Children worked in coal mines, textile mills, canneries, glass factories, and on farms, especially in poorer and immigrant communities where every extra wage mattered.

A U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics history notes that between 1890 and 1910, about 18% of all children ages 10–15 worked. Government investigations documented long hours, low pay, and serious risks to health and schooling. Childhood, for many, was not primarily about school and play; it was about production and survival.

Timeline of Child Labor

1890–1910: Industrial child labor peaks (mines, mills, factories). Children as young as 8 worked 12-hour days in dangerous conditions.
1910s–1930s: Reform campaigns, investigations, photography expose conditions. Lewis Hine's photographs bring national attention to the issue.
New Deal era: Federal labor standards raise minimum working ages and limit hours. Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 establishes national child labor protections.

Seeing the Children: Lewis Hine and Visual Shock

Statistics alone did not shift public opinion. Photographer Lewis Hine worked for the National Child Labor Committee, capturing children operating heavy machines, carrying loads, or standing outside factories late at night. His images were designed as activist tools, not just art: they made it impossible to ignore the contrast between small bodies and harsh industrial spaces.

Breaker boy, coal mine. Lewis Hine documented children as young as 8 working in dangerous coal breakers, sorting coal by hand.

"Doing Their Part": Children, War, and Patriotism

When the United States entered World War I and later World War II, earlier child-labor debates collided with wartime pressure. Children and teenagers continued to work in agriculture and certain industries, especially where family labor had always been central and regulations were weak or poorly enforced.

At the same time, wartime propaganda encouraged youth to "do their part" by working in fields, buying war stamps, or joining home-front programs. Posters framed their labor as patriotic, but beneath the flag-colored language, familiar tensions remained: families needed income, production targets had to be met, and the heaviest burdens often fell on poor, rural, or marginalized children.

"Do Your Part – At What Cost?"

Patriotic posters rallied kids to pick crops, buy stamps, and fill factory gaps.

The messaging sounded noble. In practice, low-income families stretched longer shifts, schooling halted for harvests, and dangerous piecework stayed exempt from wartime protections.

  • Seasonal farm drives pulled entire classrooms into the fields for weeks.
  • War production waivers let factories ignore hour limits with parent signatures.
  • Rural Black, Mexican, and Indigenous children filled the hardest, least-paid jobs.

Rewriting Childhood: The New Deal and Labor Laws

The Great Depression and New Deal created a new argument: why should children be working when adults were unemployed? Reformers used the crisis to push for national standards limiting child labor. Federal laws raised minimum working ages, capped hours, and banned hazardous work for minors in many industries, even though agriculture remained loosely regulated.

These changes did not erase all child labor, but they shifted the cultural baseline. Childhood became increasingly associated with school and protection rather than full-time work. On paper, the United States seemed to be moving away from treating children as just another part of the labor force.

Beyond Borders: Child Soldiers and American Power

The story does not end at U.S. borders. Today, American foreign and security policy collides with another form of child labor: children recruited into armed forces and groups. International law treats this as one of the worst forms of child labor, because it exposes children to extreme violence, coercion, and long-term physical and psychological harm.

On one hand, the United States officially recognizes this problem. The Child Soldiers Prevention Act (CSPA) of 2008 is supposed to restrict certain types of military assistance and arms sales to governments that recruit or use child soldiers. The State Department publishes annual lists of such countries.

On the other hand, research from policy organizations shows that U.S. presidents have repeatedly used waivers to keep assistance flowing to many of these same governments, citing national-security interests. One study estimates that about 97% of arms sales and assistance that could have been blocked under the CSPA has gone ahead anyway. This means U.S. policy often helps sustain the environments in which children are pulled into conflict.

Two Histories, One Pattern

Putting these histories together changes how the story of child labor in wartime looks. At home, the United States eventually labeled industrial child labor a moral and political problem and passed laws to restrict it. Abroad, the same country still makes policy choices that, according to experts, help maintain conflicts where children are used as soldiers.

The pattern is not just hypocrisy; it is about what gets treated as non-negotiable. In both cases, children's lives and futures become flexible when they collide with economic or strategic priorities: production then, security now.

Conclusion: What Does It Mean to Protect Childhood?

Expert research on child labor and health shows that its harms are ongoing: physical strain, injury, psychological stress, disrupted education. Whether in fields, factories, or armed groups, children pay high costs when their work is treated as necessary for someone else's goals.

At the same time, debates about waivers, arms exports, and child soldiers are still active in policy circles. The United States has already shown that it can rethink childhood once, shifting from an economy that normalizes child labor to a model that promises education and protection—at least within its own borders.

That raises the project's central question again: will that same shift in thinking ever fully extend beyond U.S. borders? Ending "child labor in wartime" cannot just mean pushing it out of sight to other countries. It means refusing to treat any child's life as an acceptable cost of production or security.
